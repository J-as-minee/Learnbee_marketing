# Course B: Not Yet: The One-Word Shift That Changes Everything

**Tagline:** One word. Four letters. It changes everything.
**Audience:** All employees / general learners
**Objective:** Use the word "yet" to reframe setbacks, challenges, and feedback — and understand how this one shift builds resilience over time.
**Duration:** ~15 minutes
**Language:** English

---

## Module 1: The Weight of "Can't"

### Slide 1 — Title Slide
**Title:** Not Yet: The One-Word Shift That Changes Everything
**Tagline:** One word. Four letters. It changes everything.

---

### Slide 2 — Big Statement
"I can't do this." Three words that shut a door — quietly, completely, and usually without you noticing.

---

### Slide 3 — Key Points: What "Can't" Actually Does
**Title:** The Language You Use Shapes the Brain You Have

- **It signals an ending:** "Can't" tells your brain the situation is resolved. There's nothing left to do. No path forward exists.
- **It removes agency:** Once you've said "I can't," the next logical step is to stop trying — not to ask what you could do differently.
- **It sounds like a fact:** That's the dangerous part. "I can't do this" feels like an observation, not a choice. But it is a choice.
- **It's contagious:** Say it enough, and the people around you start to believe it too — about themselves, about their work, about what's possible.

---

### Slide 4 — Image + Content (Right): The Science Behind the Word
**Title:** Your Brain Takes Your Words Literally
**Content:**
Carol Dweck's research showed that the words people use about their own abilities are more predictive of their future performance than their actual skill level at the time.

When we say "I can't," our brain treats it as a closed case — it stops generating solutions. When we say "I can't yet," the brain stays in problem-solving mode. That one word keeps the door open.

This isn't positive thinking. It's how the brain processes language. What you say about yourself becomes the instruction your brain follows.

---

### Slide 5 — True / False
**Question:** "I can't do this" is a neutral observation about your current ability.
**Answer:** False
**Explanation:** It's not neutral — it's a closed statement that shuts down further thinking. "I can't do this yet" is the more accurate (and more useful) version — it describes where you are now without deciding where you'll stay.

## Module 2: The One-Word Reframe

### Slide 6 — Big Statement
One word. Four letters. "Yet." It doesn't change what you can do right now. It changes what your brain believes is possible next.

---

### Slide 7 — Accordion: "Yet" in Four Real Situations
**Title:** Hear the Difference

**Item 1 — When you're struggling with a skill:**
Without yet: "I'm not good at public speaking."
With yet: "I'm not good at public speaking yet — what's one thing I can practise this week?"

**Item 2 — After tough feedback:**
Without yet: "My manager doesn't think I'm leadership material."
With yet: "I'm not where I need to be yet — what specifically do I need to develop?"

**Item 3 — When learning something new:**
Without yet: "I don't understand how to use this system."
With yet: "I don't understand this yet — who can help me get started?"

**Item 4 — When comparing yourself to others:**
Without yet: "They're just naturally better at this than me."
With yet: "They're ahead of me yet — what are they doing that I could learn from?"

---

### Slide 8 — Flip Cards: The Reframe in Action
**Title:** Flip the Statement

**Card 1:**
Front: "I'm not a numbers person."
Back: "I'm not confident with numbers yet. That's exactly why I should spend more time with them — not less."

**Card 2:**
Front: "I always freeze under pressure."
Back: "I haven't learned to manage pressure well yet. That's a skill, and skills can be built."

**Card 3:**
Front: "I'm terrible at giving feedback."
Back: "I haven't developed my feedback skills yet. What would it look like to practise once this week?"

**Card 4:**
Front: "I could never do what they do."
Back: "I can't do what they do yet. What would be the first step toward it?"

---

### Slide 9 — Quiz MCQ: Check Your Understanding
**Question:** What does adding "yet" to a fixed-mindset statement actually do?
- A) Makes it sound more optimistic
- B) Keeps the brain in problem-solving mode instead of closing the case ✅
- C) Changes the facts of the situation
- D) Guarantees you'll eventually succeed

**Explanation:** "Yet" doesn't promise success — it keeps possibility open. That's enough to change how the brain responds to the challenge.

## Module 3: Making It Stick

### Slide 10 — Big Statement
The real test isn't using "yet" when things are fine. It's using it at the exact moment you feel like giving up.

---

### Slide 11 — Scenario Challenge: The Hard Moment
**Scenario:** You've been working on a new skill for three months. You're still not as good as your colleague who picked it up in three weeks. Your manager asks how it's going and you feel the urge to say "I'm just not cut out for this." What do you do?

- **Option A:** Say exactly that — "I'm just not cut out for this." Tell your manager honestly that you don't think you can get there. — *This closes the conversation and your own thinking. It's the fixed voice winning.*
- **Option B:** Say "I'm not where I want to be yet — can we talk about what I could do differently?" ✅ — *"Yet" keeps the door open. It also models honesty without resignation.*
- **Option C:** Say nothing and hope the manager moves on. — *Avoidance isn't the same as a growth response. It just delays the moment of reckoning.*

---

### Slide 12 — Key Points: Four Ways to Build the Habit
**Title:** Making "Yet" Second Nature

- **Catch the "can't" in real time:** When you hear yourself say "I can't" or "I'm not," pause. Don't judge it — just add "yet" and notice if the next thought changes.
- **Write it down:** Keep a running note of the things you've said you "can't" do. Revisit it in 90 days. You'll be surprised how many of them are "cans" by then.
- **Use it with others:** When someone on your team says "I can't do this," try responding with "not yet — what would help?" It changes the conversation for both of you.
- **Start small:** Pick one "can't" this week. Just one. Reframe it with "yet" and take one concrete action toward it. That's the whole practice.

---

### Slide 13 — Quiz MCQ: Final Check
**Question:** Someone on your team says "I'm not good at this and never will be." The most growth-minded response is:
- A) "Yes, some people just have different strengths."
- B) "Don't be so hard on yourself — you're great!"
- C) "You're not there yet — what would help you get closer?" ✅
- D) "Maybe this isn't the right role for you."

**Explanation:** Growth mindset in response to others isn't about reassurance — it's about redirecting toward agency and possibility. "Not yet — what would help?" does exactly that.
