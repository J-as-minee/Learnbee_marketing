# LearnBee — In-Depth Site & Product Analysis

Sites analyzed: https://www.learnbee.com/ (marketing site), https://create.learnbee.com/welcome (account gateway), and https://dev.learnbee.ai/ (a live build of the actual product, explored hands-on). Analysis date: June 22, 2026.

## Overview

LearnBee is an AI content-authoring studio for corporate Learning & Development teams. Where Bsharp Converse (bsharpcorp.com) is about *delivering* training to frontline staff through chat and daily micro-learning, LearnBee is about *producing* the training content in the first place — turning a document, a topic, or a blank page into a polished, narrated, multi-language, interactive course in minutes. The two products share a parent company (the footer on learnbee.com reads "© 2026 Bsharp Corp," the same entity behind Bsharp Converse), and LearnBee is functionally a much deeper, standalone version of the "Content Authoring" feature card that appears on the Converse homepage.

The three URLs investigated turn out to be three different layers of the same product, not three different products: learnbee.com is the public pitch, create.learnbee.com is the sign-up/login gateway into the real app, and dev.learnbee.ai is a working development build of that same app — already logged in, with a real test course sitting in it — which made it possible to open the actual editor and the learner-facing player and see how the product behaves rather than just how it's described.

## www.learnbee.com — The Marketing Site

This is a short, single-page site with none of the multi-menu navigation seen on bsharpcorp.com — just a logo and a single "Get Started" button in the header, repeated as a "Get a Demo" option in the hero. The brevity itself is a signal: this is a narrower, single-purpose product page, not a platform with many sub-products to navigate between.

The page is built around three creation paths, presented as the product's core pitch: converting an existing document (PDF, Word file, or slide deck) directly into a structured course with slides and an auto-generated quiz; generating a course from a topic alone, with the AI writing the text, sourcing images, and producing a voiceover; and authoring a course manually, slide by slide, for teams who want full control. Beneath that, the page lists the building blocks in plain L&D language: six content/slide types (text, video, audio, image, quiz, and poll), AI voiceover in more than twenty languages that automatically re-syncs whenever a slide's text is edited, a quiz engine with configurable timers, point values, attempt limits, answer explanations, and a confidence-rating option, a choice between widescreen (16:9) and vertical/mobile (9:16) layouts, branching logic that can route a learner down a different path depending on how they answer a question, and a reporting layer that assigns content to cohorts, exports results, and surfaces knowledge gaps. The page closes on the line "Content Studio For Your L&D Team" with the same Get Started / Get a Demo button pair, then a minimal footer with a LinkedIn link and the Bsharp Corp copyright line.

Stylistically the language mirrors bsharpcorp.com's playbook — short benefit-first headlines, paired primary/secondary buttons — but compressed into a single scroll because there is only one product to sell here, not an ecosystem.

## create.learnbee.com — The Account Gateway

Clicking "Get Started" leads to create.learnbee.com/welcome, a clean, illustrated onboarding screen reading "Welcome to the Learnbee community," with a primary "Join us" button, a secondary "I'm an existing user" link, and a Google sign-in option below an "Or" divider. This is the production front door to the actual application — the point where a visitor stops reading about the product and starts an account. No further exploration was done past this screen, since proceeding would mean creating an account or signing in, which wasn't necessary to understand the product (and wasn't done without checking in first).

## dev.learnbee.ai — The Actual Product

This domain is not marketing content at all — it is a working build of the LearnBee application itself, and it loaded already signed in, with one real test course already sitting in the workspace. That made it possible to open the dashboard, the course editor, and the learner-facing player directly.

The branding here is visibly different from the polished blue-hexagon "LearnBee" logo used on the marketing site and the create.learnbee.com gateway — this build uses a plain, hand-written cursive "learnbee" wordmark on a plain white header. Combined with the pre-authenticated session and the single placeholder test course, this points to dev.learnbee.ai being an internal development or staging environment the product team uses to build and test the app, rather than a domain end users are meant to land on. It is, in effect, a backstage pass into the same product that's eventually served to real users through create.learnbee.com (or whatever the production app domain is).

The dashboard's own tagline is "Impressive, impactful training made easy," with a search bar over content and a single large tile labeled "Content — Author SCORM-ready interactive courses." That line is the most concrete fact found anywhere across both domains: LearnBee's output is packaged to the SCORM standard, meaning a finished course isn't locked into LearnBee's own player — it can be exported and dropped into essentially any corporate LMS (Bsharp Converse or otherwise). The marketing site never uses the word "SCORM"; it's the kind of detail aimed at an L&D buyer's procurement checklist rather than a marketing headline, and it only surfaced by being inside the actual app. A small "Access code" field sits in the header next to the main "New" button, suggesting the product also supports redeeming a shared or licensed code to unlock content or a workspace, separate from the normal account login.

### The Authoring Studio

Opening the one test course already in the workspace — a finance-training module titled "Derivatives, From Zero: Forwards, Futures, Options & Risk" — revealed the editor in full. Content is organized into modules (an "Opening" module and a main content module in the sample course), each holding a list of slides, and the variety of slide templates available is considerably richer than the six types named on the marketing page: alongside Title Slide and Quiz, the sidebar showed Big Statement, Image + Content, Accordion, Step by Step, Key Points, Sticky Scroll, Scenario Challenge, and Insight Cards. The marketing copy's "six content types" appears to be a simplified buyer-facing summary; the real product is a much deeper template library for varying how a given piece of information is presented.

The main canvas shows a live, editable preview of the selected slide — for the title slide, fields for title, byline/tagline, author, duration, language, and date sit directly on the slide art, plus a layout-shuffle control offering multiple visual arrangements of the same slide type. The panel on the right is contextual to whatever is selected: an Accordion slide exposes its title, which item opens by default, and a background colour, while every slide type carries its own "Narration" control, confirming that voiceover is generated and attached per slide rather than once for the whole course. The top toolbar carries the course title (editable inline), a language selector, a settings icon, an "AI Assist" button, "Preview," and a "Generate" menu with two actions — "Write Up," which flagged that a number of slides in the sample course still needed narration scripts written, and "Voice Over," which generates the spoken audio once a script exists. This two-step pipeline (write the script, then voice it) matches the marketing page's claim of AI voiceover that re-syncs on edit, but shows the actual mechanism: text generation and audio generation are separate, on-demand steps rather than one button.

### The Learner-Facing Player

Clicking "Preview" jumps to a separate player view, confirming the course has a distinct learner-facing mode rather than just an editor. The player shows a slim top progress bar and a slide counter ("5 / 14"), with Back and Next controls at the bottom and the current module name shown as a breadcrumb. The Accordion slide carried over into the player with small coloured status dots next to each item — green once opened, purple while still unopened — and the Next button stayed disabled until every item had been opened. That single detail confirms the product's "interactive, not just slides" pitch is mechanically enforced rather than cosmetic: a learner cannot progress through certain slide types without actually engaging with every element on them.

## How the Pieces Fit Together

Putting all three layers together, LearnBee is best understood as a content-creation engine spun out from the same parent company that builds Bsharp Converse, aimed at a different point in the training pipeline. Converse's "Content Authoring" feature card is a glimpse of the same idea at small scale, embedded inside a broader delivery platform; LearnBee is that idea built out as its own product, with a much larger catalogue of slide templates, a real branching/quiz engine, per-slide multilingual narration, and SCORM packaging so the output isn't tied to using Converse (or any single LMS) to deliver it. learnbee.com exists to sell that idea to an L&D buyer in the simplest possible terms; create.learnbee.com is where that buyer actually signs up; and dev.learnbee.ai is the team's own working copy of the underlying app, which — by virtue of already being logged in with a real test course inside it — ended up being the most informative of the three, because it showed the product actually functioning rather than describing it.
