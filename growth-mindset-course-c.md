# Course C: Growth Mindset at Work: Leading and Learning in Teams

**Tagline:** A team's mindset isn't built on posters. It's built on how you respond on a Tuesday afternoon.
**Audience:** Managers, team leads, senior individual contributors
**Objective:** Understand how team culture is shaped by leader behaviour, learn to give feedback that fosters growth, and take practical steps toward building psychological safety in your team.
**Duration:** ~20 minutes
**Language:** English

## Module 1: The Team Mindset Is Set at the Top

### Slide 1 — Title Slide
**Title:** Growth Mindset at Work: Leading and Learning in Teams
**Tagline:** A team's mindset isn't built on posters. It's built on how you respond on a Tuesday afternoon.

---

### Slide 2 — Big Statement
A team's mindset isn't the sum of individual mindsets. It's shaped — almost entirely — by whoever leads them.

---

### Slide 3 — Image + Content (Left): The Research That Changed Management
**Title:** Psychological Safety: What Google Found
**Content:**
In 2012, Google ran a major internal study to find out what made its best teams great. They looked at everything — backgrounds, seniority, communication styles, meeting frequency.

None of it mattered as much as one thing: **psychological safety** — whether team members felt safe to take risks, speak up, and make mistakes without being humiliated or punished.

Amy Edmondson at Harvard had identified this concept years earlier. What Google confirmed was that psychological safety isn't a personality trait of individual employees. It's a product of the leader's behaviour. Teams don't feel safe — or unsafe — randomly. They follow the leader's lead.

---

### Slide 4 — Key Points: Fixed vs. Growth Team Cultures
**Title:** Two Kinds of Teams

- **In a fixed-mindset team:** Mistakes are hidden, not discussed. People compete instead of collaborate. "Looking good" matters more than learning. Feedback flows downward and is received as judgement.
- **In a growth-mindset team:** Mistakes are debriefed openly. People share what isn't working. Learning is treated as part of the job. Feedback flows in all directions and is received as information.
- **The visible difference:** You can feel it in the first five minutes of a team meeting. Are people speaking up or staying quiet? Are problems surfaced early or discovered late?
- **The invisible driver:** The leader sets this culture — not through mission statements, but through how they respond when something goes wrong.

---

### Slide 5 — Quiz MCQ: Read the Room
**Question:** Your team missed a project deadline. In a growth-mindset team culture, what happens next?
- A) The manager identifies who's responsible and addresses it privately.
- B) The team discusses what happened, what they learned, and what changes next time. ✅
- C) The manager reassures everyone that it's fine and moves on quickly.
- D) Nothing — the team knows to do better next time without being told.

**Explanation:** Growth-minded teams debrief openly. Option A is blame-focused, C avoids learning, D assumes learning happens without structure. A proper retrospective — even a 20-minute one — is what growth cultures actually look like.

## Module 2: How You Respond Shapes Everything

### Slide 6 — Big Statement
The way you respond after someone makes a mistake tells your entire team — not just that person — exactly how much risk they're allowed to take.

---

### Slide 7 — Accordion: Four Manager Moments That Define Culture
**Title:** Watch What You Do After These

**Item 1 — After a mistake:**
Fixed response: "How did this happen? Who signed off on this?" — Team learns: hide mistakes early, escalate late.
Growth response: "Let's understand what went wrong and what we'd do differently. What do we need to change?" — Team learns: surface problems early, debrief honestly.

**Item 2 — After someone shares a bad idea:**
Fixed response: Visible dismissal, moving on quickly, or polite silence. — Team learns: don't share half-formed ideas. Only speak when you're sure.
Growth response: "Interesting — what's the problem you're trying to solve? Let's build on that." — Team learns: ideas are drafts, not finished products. It's safe to think out loud.

**Item 3 — After giving feedback:**
Fixed response: "Just make sure it doesn't happen again." — Team learns: feedback is a verdict, not a conversation.
Growth response: "Here's what I saw. What was your read on it? What would you do differently?" — Team learns: feedback is a dialogue that makes both people smarter.

**Item 4 — After someone exceeds expectations:**
Fixed response: Praise for the result: "Great work — that was impressive." — Team learns: performance is about outcomes. Being impressive is the goal.
Growth response: Praise for the process: "Really good work. What did you do differently this time?" — Team learns: the method matters. Growth is the goal.

---

### Slide 8 — Scenario Challenge: The Missed Target
**Scenario:** A team member comes to you and says they've missed a key target this quarter — one they were confident about. They look embarrassed and are clearly bracing for a difficult conversation. How do you respond?

- **Option A:** "That's disappointing. Let's make sure this doesn't happen again next quarter." — *Signals that results matter more than understanding. The team member learns to hide problems next time rather than bring them to you early.*
- **Option B:** "Thanks for telling me. Let's figure out what happened — what do you think got in the way?" ✅ — *Opens a genuine debrief. Shows that bringing you bad news early is safe. This is the response that builds the culture you want.*
- **Option C:** "It's fine — one bad quarter isn't the end of the world." — *Kind, but it avoids the learning. They don't come away knowing what to do differently.*

---

### Slide 9 — Flip Cards: Fixed vs. Growth Manager Responses
**Title:** Which Manager Are You?

**Card 1:**
Front: Your team member asks a question you think they should already know the answer to.
Back: **Fixed:** A visible sigh, a short answer, moving on quickly. **Growth:** "Good question — let me show you how I think about it." The second response invites more questions next time.

**Card 2:**
Front: Someone pushes back on your decision in a team meeting.
Back: **Fixed:** Shut it down or take it personally. **Growth:** "Tell me more about your thinking." Even if you keep the decision, you've signalled that challenge is welcome.

**Card 3:**
Front: A project fails despite the team's best efforts.
Back: **Fixed:** Focus on what went wrong and who was responsible. **Growth:** "What did we learn, and what would we do differently?" The debrief is where growth lives.

**Card 4:**
Front: A high performer tells you they want to move into a new area they have no experience in.
Back: **Fixed:** Redirect them to what they're already good at — you need them there. **Growth:** "What's driving that? Let's figure out how to create some exposure." Investing in their growth builds loyalty and capability.

## Module 3: Building a Learning Culture

### Slide 10 — Big Statement
A learning culture isn't declared in a strategy document. It's built in the small moments — how you run your one-to-ones, how you start your retrospectives, how you respond when someone admits they don't know.

---

### Slide 11 — Step by Step: Four Practices to Start This Week
**Title:** From Intention to Habit

**Step 1 — Open your next team meeting with a learning question:**
Instead of "what's the update on X?", try: "What's something we got wrong recently that we learned from?" or "What's one thing that surprised you this week?" Signal from the start that learning is on the agenda.

**Step 2 — Run a 20-minute retrospective after your next project:**
Three questions: What went well? What didn't? What do we do differently next time? Don't skip this when things go well — learning from success is just as important.

**Step 3 — Give process praise, not just outcome praise:**
When someone does well, ask what they did differently. "That was great — walk me through your thinking." It reinforces the behaviour, not just the result.

**Step 4 — Model being wrong:**
Share a mistake you made recently — out loud, with what you learned from it. One act of leader vulnerability changes the team's felt permission to be honest. Do it once. Notice what happens.

---

### Slide 12 — Insight Cards: Signs Your Team Has a Fixed Culture
**Title:** Honest Signals to Watch For

**Card 1 — Problems arrive late:**
If your team consistently brings you issues after they've become crises — not before — it's a sign that raising problems early feels unsafe.

**Card 2 — Meetings are performative:**
If team members say "everything's on track" in every meeting but then struggle privately, the meeting has become a performance, not a conversation.

**Card 3 — Blame travels fast, learning travels slow:**
After something goes wrong, if the first question is "whose fault is it?" rather than "what happened?", the culture is fixed-minded around failure.

**Card 4 — People stop asking questions:**
When team members stop asking "why" or "how does this work" — especially in front of peers — they've learned that not knowing is something to hide.

---

### Slide 13 — Key Points: What to Do if You Recognise Your Team
**Title:** If You Saw Your Team in Those Cards

- **Don't announce a culture change:** Telling your team "we're going to have a growth mindset now" signals the problem without solving it. Culture changes through behaviour, not declarations.
- **Start with yourself:** Pick one of the four practices from Module 3 and do it this week. The team will notice before you say a word.
- **Repair specific moments:** If you've been dismissive about questions or punishing about mistakes, you can repair that directly — "I realise I haven't made it easy to bring me problems early. I want to change that." One honest sentence goes a long way.
- **Measure by behaviour, not belief:** You'll know the culture is shifting when people start surfacing problems earlier, asking more questions in meetings, and debriefing honestly after setbacks. That's the proof.

---

### Slide 14 — Quiz MCQ: Final Check
**Question:** A growth-minded manager responds to a team member's mistake by:
- A) Making it clear the standard wasn't met and setting expectations for next time.
- B) Reassuring them it's not a big deal and moving on quickly.
- C) Running a short debrief — what happened, what we'd do differently, what we learned. ✅
- D) Escalating to HR to make sure it's documented.

**Explanation:** Growth leadership turns mistakes into learning events. Neither punishment (A) nor avoidance (B) does that. A short, honest debrief is the practice that builds both capability and culture.
