## Module 1 — Meet the colony

### Slide 1 — Title slide
**Title:** The world of bees
**Subtitle:** Inside the hive that runs on teamwork, dance, and 5,000 flower visits a day.
**Background:** vibrant macro photo of a bee on a flower
**Narration:** "Welcome to The world of bees. By the end of this, you'll understand the tiny society that pollinates a third of everything you eat."

### Slide 2 — Agenda slide
**Title:** What's ahead
1. Meet the colony — who's who inside the hive
2. Inside the hive — how bees talk and make honey
3. Why bees matter — pollination, and what's at stake

**Narration:** "Three stops: who's in the hive, how they run it, and why it matters far beyond the hive itself."

### Slide 3 — Big statement
**Headline:** A single bee visits up to 5,000 flowers a day.
**Subtext:** And she remembers every good one.
**Narration:** "Five thousand flowers, one bee, one day. And she'll remember the best ones tomorrow."

### Slide 4 — Key points
**Title:** Three jobs, one hive
- **Queen** — The only job is to lay eggs, up to 2,000 a day.
- **Worker bees** — Female, and they do everything else: nursing, building, guarding, foraging.
- **Drones** — Male, with one job (mate with a queen), and they don't even have stingers.

**Narration:** "Every hive runs on three roles. One queen, thousands of workers, and a handful of drones who exist for a single purpose."

### Slide 5 — Insight cards *(click mode)*
**Title:** What makes a bee a bee
1. **Compound eyes** — Five eyes total: two big compound ones, three simple ones on top, built to track fast motion and ultraviolet light.
2. **Pollen baskets** — Built-in pouches on her hind legs carry pollen home, sometimes a third of her own body weight.
3. **The stinger** — Only females have one. She can only use it once, then she's gone.

**Narration (per card):**
- "Look at those eyes, five in total, built for catching motion humans can't even see."
- "That fuzzy leg isn't fuzzy by accident, it's a basket for hauling pollen home."
- "Only female bees sting. And for a worker bee, that sting is also her last act."

### Slide 6 — Image + content (left)
**Title:** The queen's only job
**Body:** She's the biggest bee in the hive, not because she's in charge, but because she's the only one who can do this job. A healthy queen lays up to 2,000 eggs a day, more than her own body weight, every single day of peak season.
**Image:** a marked queen bee surrounded by workers
**Narration:** "She's not bossing anyone around. She's just laying eggs, all day, every day, more than her own weight in eggs, daily."

### Slide 7 — Flip cards *(click mode)*
**Title:** Worker bee, many hats
1. Week 1: Nurse → Feeds and cleans the larvae, over 1,000 visits a day, per larva.
2. Week 2: Builder → Builds wax comb cell by cell, using wax made in her own body.
3. Week 3: Guard → Checks every bee at the door by scent before letting them in.
4. Week 4+: Forager → Finally leaves the hive, and flies up to 5 miles round trip for food.

**Narration:** "Flip it. A worker bee's job changes every single week of her life, she's basically four employees in one."

### Slide 8 — Comparison side-by-side
**Title:** Worker vs. drone
**Worker:** Female · has a stinger · forages, builds, guards · lives 5–6 weeks
**Drone:** Male · no stinger · only job is mating · often doesn't survive mating

**Narration:** "Put them side by side and the difference is stark, one does everything, the other does exactly one thing, once."

## Module 2 — Inside the hive

### Slide 9 — Big statement *(video mode)*
**Caption:** Bees dance to talk.
**Video:** short clip of a waggle dance / close-up bee movement — narration auto-disables in video mode, the video owns the audio
**On-screen support text:** Direction = where. Speed = how far. This is a real conversation.

### Slide 10 — Step-by-step *(gated, click mode)*
**Title:** How honey actually gets made
1. **Collect** — A forager fills her honey stomach with nectar, a second stomach just for transport.
2. **Pass it on** — Back at the hive, she passes the nectar mouth-to-mouth to a house bee, again and again.
3. **Add enzymes** — Each pass adds enzymes that start breaking sugars down.
4. **Fan it dry** — Worker bees fan the wet nectar with their wings until it thickens into honey.

**Narration (per step):**
1. "It starts with a second stomach, yes really, built just for carrying nectar home."
2. "Back at the hive, that nectar gets passed mouth to mouth, bee to bee."
3. "Every pass adds enzymes that start turning sugar into honey."
4. "Then it's just fanning, thousands of tiny wings, drying it down to honey."

### Slide 11 — Sticky scroll *(gated)*
**Title:** Inside a honeycomb
1. Every cell is a perfect hexagon, no wasted wax, no wasted space.
2. Bees build at a 13-degree upward tilt, so honey never spills out.
3. A single comb can hold over 2 pounds of honey before it's capped.

**Narration:** matches each line above, one per pinned step.

### Slide 12 — Accordion *(gated, click mode)*
**Title:** Bee myths, busted
1. Myth: All bees sting. → Fact: Only female bees can sting, and many species don't sting at all.
2. Myth: Bees die after one sting. → Fact: Only honeybees do, most other bees can sting repeatedly.
3. Myth: Bees are aggressive. → Fact: Bees only sting to defend the hive. Out foraging, they couldn't care less about you.
4. Myth: Killing one bee is harmless. → Fact: A dying honeybee releases an alarm scent that calls in defenders.

**Narration:** matches each "fact" line above, read on expand.

### Slide 13 — Image match *(gated)*
**Title:** Match the bee to her job
- Nurse bee photo ↔ "Feeds the larvae"
- Builder bee photo ↔ "Shapes the wax comb"
- Guard bee photo ↔ "Checks the door"
- Forager bee photo ↔ "Brings home the food"

**Narration:** "You've met all four. Now match them to their job."

### Slide 14 — Fill in the blanks *(gated)*
**Title:** Complete the fact
**Sentence:** A bee's waggle dance tells the rest of the hive ___ and ___ to a food source.
**Answers:** direction, distance

**Narration:** "Fill in the blank, you just learned this two slides ago."

### Slide 15 — Image explore *(gated, click mode)*
**Title:** Anatomy of a worker bee
**Background:** labeled bee photo/diagram
**Hotspots:**
1. **Antennae** — Used to smell, taste, and even feel vibration, she "hears" through her antennae, not ears.
2. **Pollen basket** — A built-in pouch on her hind leg for hauling pollen home.
3. **Stinger** — Barbed, and only used once. She dies after using it.
4. **Wings** — Beat 230 times per second, that's the buzz you hear.

**Narration:** matches each hotspot fact above.

## Module 3 — Why bees matter

### Slide 16 — Comparison matrix
**Title:** Honeybee vs. bumblebee vs. wasp

| | Size | Stings repeatedly? | Makes honey? | Social structure |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Honeybee | Small | No (dies after one sting) | Yes | Highly social, tens of thousands |
| Bumblebee | Large, fuzzy | Yes | No, small amounts only | Social, dozens to hundreds |
| Wasp | Slim, smooth | Yes | No | Varies, often solitary |

**Narration:** "Three look-alikes, three very different lives. Only one of them makes the honey you actually eat."

### Slide 17 — Comparison pros & cons
**Title:** Wild hives vs. managed hives
**Wild:** Pros — natural behavior, genetic diversity. Cons — vulnerable to disease, harder to track.
**Managed:** Pros — protected, monitored, supports farming. Cons — can spread disease between colonies if not careful.

**Narration:** "Neither is perfect, but together, they're how most of the world's pollination actually happens."

### Slide 18 — Image overlay (right)
**Title:** One in three bites
**Overlay text:** About a third of everything you eat exists because a bee visited a flower first.
**Image:** produce market or fruit stand
**Narration:** "Look at your next meal. A third of it, roughly, only exists because a bee did her job."

### Slide 19 — Sticky slide *(gated)*
**Title:** From flower to fruit
1. A bee lands, picks up pollen by accident, brushing against the flower's anthers.
2. She carries it to the next flower while looking for more nectar.
3. Pollen meets pistil. Fertilization happens.
4. Weeks later: fruit, or a seed for the next generation.

**Narration:** matches each step above.

### Slide 20 — Scenario challenge *(gated)*
**Setup:** You find a swarm of bees resting on a tree branch in your backyard.
**Question:** What do you do?

| Option | Feedback |
|---|---|
| A. Spray it with insecticide | Wrong move. A resting swarm is calm and temporary, and you'd be killing a colony that helps your whole neighborhood. |
| **B. Call a local beekeeper or swarm removal service** | **Correct.** Most beekeepers will relocate a swarm for free, they're happy to have free bees. |
| C. Poke it with a stick to see what happens | Please don't. A calm swarm can become a defensive one fast. |
| D. Ignore it and hope it leaves | It might move on its own in a day or two, but calling a beekeeper is faster and safer for everyone. |

**Narration (intro):** "Here's a real one. A swarm shows up in your yard, what's your actual move?"

### Slide 21 — Quiz MCQ *(gated)*
1. **How many eggs can a healthy queen lay in a day?** A) 200 B) **2,000 ✓** C) 20,000 — "More than her own body weight, daily."
2. **What carries the message in a waggle dance?** A) Sound B) Scent C) **Movement and angle ✓** — "It's the angle and duration of the dance, not sound."
3. **Which bee has no stinger?** A) Queen B) Worker C) **Drone ✓** — "Drones are male and stingerless, their only job is mating."

### Slide 22 — True/false quick-fire *(gated)*
1. All bees make honey. → **False** — Only honeybees do, in meaningful amounts.
2. A honeybee dies after stinging once. → **True** — The barbed stinger stays behind.
3. Bees can recognize human faces. → **True** — Studies show honeybees can learn and recognize individual human faces.

### Slide 23 — Key points (recap)
**Title:** Three things to remember
- One hive runs on three roles, queen, worker, drone, and only one of them lays eggs.
- Bees talk through dance, not sound, direction and distance, encoded in movement.
- About a third of your food exists because a bee visited a flower first.

**Narration:** "Three things worth keeping: the roles, the dance, and the one-in-three bite."

### Slide 24 — Big statement (closing)
**Headline:** Save the bees. Save the harvest.
**Subtext:** You now know more about bees than most people ever will.
**Narration:** "Save the bees, save the harvest, and you now know exactly why that's not just a bumper sticker."
**Completion screen:** pass mark across the quiz/true-false/scenario slides; closing line: "Course complete. Go tell someone about the waggle dance."
