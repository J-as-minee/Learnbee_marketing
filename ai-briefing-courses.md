## Module 1 — What AI Actually Is

**1.1 · title-slide**
- Title: "AI in Plain English"
- Subtitle: "No hype. No jargon. Just what it is and how to use it."
- Narration: "Welcome to AI in Plain English. By the end of this course, you'll understand what AI tools actually are, what they're genuinely good at, and where they quietly fail."

**1.2 · big-statement**
- Statement: "AI doesn't think. It predicts."
- Narration: "This is the most important thing to understand about AI. It doesn't reason, it doesn't understand, and it doesn't know anything. It predicts the most statistically likely next word, given everything it's seen. That's it. Once you get this, everything else makes sense."

**1.3 · key-points**
- Heading: "How a large language model actually works"
- Points:
  1. It was trained on enormous amounts of text — books, websites, code, conversations — scraped from the internet
  2. During training, it learned to predict: given this text, what word comes next?
  3. Do that billions of times and the model starts to look like it understands language — because it's very good at the patterns
  4. When you type a prompt, it's not searching the internet or reasoning — it's completing your text, very convincingly
  5. Everything it does — answering questions, writing emails, summarising documents — is pattern-matching at massive scale
- Narration: "Large language models are next-word prediction engines, trained on more text than a human could read in a thousand lifetimes. The result is something that sounds fluent and often looks intelligent — but operates very differently from the way a human does."

**1.4 · comparison-side-by-side**
- Left — "What AI is": A pattern-matching system trained on text. Very good at generating fluent language, summarising, translating, reformatting, drafting, and brainstorming. Fast, tireless, and available instantly.
- Right — "What AI isn't": A search engine. A database of facts. A reasoning system. Something that checks its own work. Something that knows what's true. Something that remembers your last conversation by default.
- Narration: "The confusion about AI comes from mixing up what it is with what it seems like. It seems like it's looking things up — it isn't. It seems like it's reasoning — it mostly isn't. Knowing the difference changes how you use it."

**1.5 · true-false**
- Statement: "AI understands the meaning of what it says."
- Answer: False — AI processes statistical patterns in text, not meaning. It can produce a flawlessly written paragraph about grief without having any concept of what grief is. This is why it can sound completely convincing while being completely wrong.
- Narration: "True or false — think about how AI actually works before you answer."

## Module 3 — Using AI Well

**3.1 · insight-cards**
- Heading: "Three principles for using AI at work"
- Card 1: "Treat every output as a first draft" — AI gets you to 70% faster than you could alone. Your job is the last 30%: the verification, the judgment, the context only you have. AI removes blank pages; it doesn't remove thinking.
- Card 2: "Give it more context than you think it needs" — AI performs proportionally to the context it receives. Tell it who you are, who the audience is, what tone you want, what you've already tried, and what good looks like. Vague prompts get vague answers.
- Card 3: "Know what it doesn't know" — Check the model's training cutoff. Don't ask it about yesterday's news, your company's internal data, or niche technical domains without verifying. The more specific and current the information, the more carefully you should check the output.
- Narration: "Three things that separate people who get consistent value from AI from people who give up after a week."

**3.2 · step-by-step** *(click mode)*
1. **Define the task clearly** — Before opening the AI tool, write one sentence: what specifically do I want? Vague tasks produce vague outputs. Narration: "Start with the task, not the tool. Know exactly what you're asking for before you type."
2. **Give it context** — Who are you? Who is the audience? What's the tone, format, length? What are the constraints? The more context, the better the output. Narration: "Context is the difference between a generic response and something actually useful."
3. **Treat the first response as a draft** — Read it critically. What's wrong? What's missing? What needs to be verified? Don't publish or send the first output. Narration: "The first output is the starting point. Your job starts when the AI finishes."
4. **Verify anything factual** — Any statistic, citation, name, date, or claim that matters should be checked against a primary source. Don't assume accuracy because the writing sounds confident. Narration: "Confidence in the writing is not evidence of accuracy in the facts. Check what matters."
5. **Iterate** — Follow up with corrections, ask it to try again, give it the feedback it needs. AI responds well to specific redirection. Narration: "One prompt is rarely the whole conversation. Treat it like working with a collaborator: give feedback, get better results."
- Narration (intro): "Here's a simple workflow that turns AI from a novelty into a reliable tool."

**3.3 · big-statement**
- Statement: "The bottleneck isn't the AI. It's knowing what to ask."
- Narration: "Everyone has access to roughly the same AI tools. The difference in outcomes isn't the tool — it's the quality of the thinking that goes into using it. AI amplifies whatever you bring to it. Bring clear thinking, and you'll get clear outputs. Bring vague questions, and you'll get impressive-sounding noise."

**1.1 · title-slide**
- Title: "Prompt Engineering 101"
- Subtitle: "How to talk to AI so it actually helps you."

## Module 2 — The Building Blocks

**2.1 · key-points**
- Heading: "The four elements of a strong prompt"
- Points:
  1. **Role** — Tell AI who it is: "You are a senior marketing strategist" or "You are a plain-English explainer for non-technical audiences." It performs better when it knows the persona it's playing.
  2. **Task** — Be specific about what you want. Not "write something about X" but "write a 200-word summary of X for a team that has no background in it."
  3. **Context** — Give the situation: who is the audience, what do they already know, what's the purpose, what have you already tried, what are the constraints?
  4. **Format** — Specify the output: bullet points or prose? How long? What headings? Should it include examples? A table? A numbered list?
- Narration: "Four ingredients. Every good prompt has all four. Most weak prompts are missing two or three of them."

**2.2 · step-by-step** *(click mode)*
1. **Start with the role** — "You are a [role] with expertise in [domain]." This frames the entire response. Narration: "Role first. It sets the tone and expertise level for everything that follows."
2. **State the task precisely** — Use action verbs: write, summarise, compare, explain, critique, rewrite, translate. Be specific about length and scope. Narration: "The task is the core instruction. Vague verbs get vague results."
3. **Add the context** — Who is this for? What do they know? What's the goal? What are you trying to avoid? Narration: "Context is where most people underinvest. The more relevant detail, the more relevant the output."
4. **Specify the format** — Bullet list? Numbered steps? Table? Under 150 words? With subheadings? State it explicitly or you'll get whatever the AI defaults to. Narration: "Format last, but don't skip it — it shapes whether you can actually use the output."
- Narration (intro): "Let's build a prompt live, one element at a time."

**2.3 · flip-cards** *(click mode)*
- Card 1: Front — "Zero-shot prompting" | Back — "Just give the instruction with no examples. Works for simple, clear tasks where the format and intent are obvious. 'Summarise this in three bullet points.' Best for: speed, when the task is self-evident."
- Card 2: Front — "Few-shot prompting" | Back — "Give 2–3 examples of the output you want before asking for the real one. AI pattern-matches to your examples. Best for: when you have a specific tone, format, or style that's hard to describe in words."
- Card 3: Front — "Chain of thought" | Back — "Ask AI to think step by step before answering. 'Walk me through your reasoning before giving me the final answer.' Best for: complex problems, analysis, or when you need to see the logic, not just the conclusion."
- Card 4: Front — "Role play / persona" | Back — "Ask AI to take on a specific persona for the whole conversation. 'For this conversation, you are a sceptical CFO reviewing a budget proposal.' Best for: stress-testing ideas, practising difficult conversations, getting contrarian feedback."
- Narration: "Four prompt patterns worth knowing. Each has a different use case — flip the cards to see when to reach for each one."

**2.4 · accordion** *(click mode)*
- Item 1: "Being too vague" — "'Write something about leadership' will produce something generic about leadership. Every word of vagueness in your prompt produces a word of vagueness in the output. Fix: add role, audience, length, and purpose."
- Item 2: "Asking multiple things in one prompt" — "'Summarise this, then critique it, then suggest improvements, then rewrite it' splits the AI's attention and produces mediocre results across all four. Fix: one clear task per prompt, then follow up."
- Item 3: "Not specifying the audience" — "AI defaults to a general audience. If you're writing for a 10-year-old, a CFO, or a technical engineer, say so — the vocabulary, examples, and depth change completely."
- Item 4: "Accepting the first response" — "The first output is a draft. If it's not right, don't start over — follow up. 'Make it shorter.' 'Change the tone to be more direct.' 'Add a specific example.' Iteration gets you to the final answer faster than reprompting from scratch."
- Item 5: "Forgetting to say what NOT to do" — "Negative constraints are powerful. 'Don't use bullet points.' 'Don't mention price.' 'Avoid jargon.' 'Don't start with the word I.' AI responds well to explicit exclusions."
- Narration: "Five mistakes that produce weak prompts — and the fix for each one."

**2.5 · quiz-mcq**
- Question: "You want AI to match a specific writing style you use for client emails. Which prompting technique works best?"
- Options: A) Zero-shot — just describe the style in words  B) Few-shot — paste in 2–3 examples of your actual emails first  C) Chain of thought — ask it to reason about your style before writing  D) Role play — tell it to be you
- Correct: B
- Narration: "Which technique fits the task — pick your answer."

## Module 3 — Prompts in Practice

**3.1 · scenario-challenge**
- Setup: "You need to brief AI to help you prepare for a difficult performance conversation with a team member who has been missing deadlines. You want realistic practice — the AI plays the team member, you play the manager. Which prompt do you use?"
- Options:
  A) "Help me prepare for a difficult conversation."
  B) "You are a team member who has missed three consecutive deadlines. You tend to be defensive when challenged. I am your manager and I want to practise this conversation. Stay in character and respond as this person would. Start by giving me a typical response when I open the conversation."
  C) "What should I say in a difficult conversation about missed deadlines?"
  D) "Write a script for a performance conversation about missed deadlines."
- Correct: B
- Feedback B: "This prompt has role, task, context, and format — and it uses the role-play technique specifically suited to conversation practice. The detail about the team member being defensive gives the AI a realistic character to play, making the practice more useful."
- Feedback A: "Too vague. AI doesn't know what kind of help you need, who's involved, or what the conversation is actually about."
- Feedback C: "This gives you advice, not practice. Knowing what to say and being able to say it under pressure are different things — the scenario calls for practice, not a script."
- Feedback D: "A script removes the dynamic unpredictability that makes conversation practice valuable. You need to respond in real time, not read lines."
- Narration: "Prompts for practice scenarios are one of the most underused AI applications. Pick the one that actually sets it up right."

**3.2 · insight-cards**
- Heading: "Three advanced prompt techniques"
- Card 1: "Ask AI to critique its own output" — After it responds, follow up with: 'What are the three weakest parts of that response, and how would you improve them?' AI will often identify its own gaps better than you can — and the revised version is usually significantly stronger.
- Card 2: "Give examples of what good looks like" — Instead of describing your ideal output, show it. 'Here's an example of the kind of email I'm happy with: [example]. Now write one in the same style for this situation: [new situation].' Examples outperform descriptions almost every time.
- Card 3: "Specify what to avoid" — Negative constraints sharpen outputs dramatically. 'Don't use corporate jargon.' 'Don't pad the answer.' 'Don't suggest options — give me a single recommendation.' Knowing what's out of scope helps AI stay inside it.
- Narration: "Three techniques that separate people who get consistently great outputs from people who get consistently mediocre ones."

**3.3 · big-statement**
- Statement: "A prompt is a brief. The better the brief, the better the work."
- Narration: "Every discipline that involves commissioning creative or analytical work — advertising, design, research, consulting — has learned that output quality is capped by brief quality. AI is no different. If you can brief a colleague clearly, you can prompt AI well. The skill is the same."

## Module 1 — The Problems Are Real

**1.1 · title-slide**
- Title: "AI Ethics: The Questions Worth Asking"
- Subtitle: "Not a philosophy lecture. A practical guide to getting AI decisions right."
- Narration: "Welcome. This course isn't about whether AI is good or bad. It's about the specific, practical questions you should be asking before you deploy it, use it, or recommend it — and what to do when the answers are complicated."

**1.2 · big-statement**
- Statement: "AI doesn't have values. But the people who build — and use — it do."
- Narration: "AI isn't neutral. It reflects the choices made by the people who built it: what data to train on, what outcomes to optimise for, what to measure and what to ignore. Understanding that is the starting point for using it responsibly."

**1.3 · key-points**
- Heading: "The five ethical risks that matter most"
- Points:
  1. **Bias** — AI trained on biased data produces biased outputs. This is not theoretical: it's happened in hiring, criminal sentencing, healthcare, and lending — with measurable harm to real people.
  2. **Hallucination** — AI states false information confidently. When that information is used in decisions — medical, legal, financial — the consequences can be serious.
  3. **Privacy** — Many AI tools are trained on or process data that wasn't consented to. Pasting confidential information into a public AI tool may mean that data is used for future training.
  4. **Accountability** — When an AI system makes a bad decision, who is responsible? The company that built it? The company that deployed it? The person who used it? This is unresolved in most organisations and most legal systems.
  5. **Transparency** — Most AI systems are black boxes. They can tell you what they concluded, not why. In high-stakes decisions, 'the AI said so' is not an acceptable explanation.
- Narration: "Five risks. All of them real. All of them already causing problems in organisations that didn't think through them in advance."

**1.4 · true-false**
- Statement: "AI is objective because it's a machine — it doesn't have opinions or prejudices."
- Answer: False — AI learns from human-generated data, which encodes human biases. A model trained on decades of hiring decisions will learn that certain profiles get hired more — and reproduce that pattern, at scale, faster than any human recruiter. Objectivity has to be designed in. It doesn't come for free.
- Narration: "True or false — one of the most common assumptions about AI."

## Module 3 — A Framework for AI Decisions

**3.1 · step-by-step** *(click mode)*
1. **What data was it trained on?** — Ask the vendor or check the documentation. Data shapes outputs. If the training data was biased, incomplete, or unrepresentative of your use case, the outputs will be too. Narration: "Start here. The training data is the foundation. Understand it before you trust the outputs."
2. **What could go wrong?** — Run a pre-mortem: imagine the AI makes a bad decision. What's the worst realistic outcome? Who is affected? This isn't pessimism — it's risk management. Narration: "Think through failure modes before you deploy, not after."
3. **Who is accountable?** — If the AI makes an error that harms someone, who is responsible? Name the person — not the system. There must be a human in the accountability chain. Narration: "Accountability can't sit with the AI. Decide who's responsible before you need to know."
4. **Could this affect someone unfairly?** — Could different outcomes occur for different demographic groups? Could this disadvantage people who are already disadvantaged? If you can't answer this confidently, find out before you proceed. Narration: "Fairness doesn't happen automatically. You have to look for unfairness — it won't announce itself."
5. **Is the output verified?** — For any high-stakes decision, a human should review and be able to explain the AI's conclusion. 'The AI recommended it' is not an explanation. Can a person stand behind this decision? Narration: "The last check. For anything that matters, a human has to own the output — not just pass it through."
- Narration (intro): "Five questions. Run them every time you're deciding whether or how to use AI in a meaningful decision."

**3.2 · comparison-side-by-side**
- Left — "Lower risk AI uses": Drafting emails and documents · Summarising long reports · Generating ideas and options · Proofreading and editing · Internal search and FAQs · Creating training materials
- Right — "Higher risk AI uses": Screening job candidates · Informing medical or clinical decisions · Predicting criminal or legal risk · Automated customer decisions (credit, insurance) · Generating content about real people · Replacing human judgment in safety-critical contexts
- Narration: "Not all AI use is equally risky. The framework from the last slide matters more as you move right. The higher the stakes, the more rigorous the scrutiny needs to be."

**3.3 · insight-cards**
- Heading: "Three things every organisation should have before deploying AI"
- Card 1: "An AI use policy" — A written policy that tells employees: what AI tools are approved, what data they can and can't input, who owns the output, and what decisions AI is not permitted to make without human review. Without this, you're managing AI use case by case, after the fact.
- Card 2: "A data classification framework" — Not all data should go into AI tools. A classification framework (public / internal / confidential / restricted) makes it clear what can be processed where — and stops the most dangerous data handling from happening by accident.
- Card 3: "A named person responsible for AI" — Someone who owns the question: what are we using AI for, is it working, is it causing harm, and are we compliant? Without a named owner, AI governance is everyone's responsibility — which means it's no one's.
